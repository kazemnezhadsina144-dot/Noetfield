In one line
Why a Trust Brief here

The institution did not need another AI strategy slide deck; it needed a short, defensible view of material AI decisions and safeguards that boards and supervisors could reuse.

Objectives

Objectives agreed with the partner

Before the pilot started, the institution and Noetfield agreed a small set of outcomes that would count as success.

  • Identify 3–10 AI-enabled decision paths that are genuinely material for risk, customers, or supervisors.
  • Produce a 12–16 page AI Trust Brief that the Board Risk Committee could read in one sitting.
  • Create a first Trust Ledger slice that could be reattached to board packs and regulatory files.
  • Surface 3–5 specific governance moves for the next 12 months, not a long wish-list.
Design choice: clarity and reuse were treated as more important than full inventory or technical exhaustiveness.
Approach

How the pilot was structured

The engagement followed the standard Trust Brief pattern – adapted to local governance cycles and language.

Overall rhythm – 6 weeks, three passes.

Weeks 1–2
Scoping and artefact sweep

Focused sessions with risk, technology, and frontline leaders to locate material AI use; collection of logs, dashboards, validation summaries, policies, and minutes; agreement on which lines of business and decision paths to cover in the first Brief.

Weeks 3–4
Mapping and draft Trust Brief

Structured mapping of selected AI decisions using the Trust Ledger schema; drafting of the AI Trust Brief in plain language, focusing on decisions, safeguards, and gaps; interim review with the sponsor group to check alignment with internal language and sensitivities.

Weeks 5–6
Refinement and governance packaging

Finalisation of the Trust Brief and its executive summary; creation of a first Trust Ledger slice covering the most significant AI decision paths; preparation of a short companion note on how the Ledger could live inside existing governance cycles.

Working style
Minimal disruption, maximum reuse

The overall time commitment for the institution was kept deliberately limited, focusing on existing artefacts rather than net-new documentation. Workshops were short, structured, and anchored to real decisions – not to abstract frameworks.

Noetfield handled the mapping and drafting workbench; the institution focused on validation, context, and calibration against internal governance language.

Trust Ledger slice

What went into the first Ledger view

The pilot did not try to map everything. It created a thin, high-signal slice of the Trust Ledger that could grow over time.

Content
Decisions and safeguards captured
  • AI-enabled credit decisions that materially affect pricing, approvals, or limits.
  • Customer interaction flows where AI materially shapes offers, nudges, or outcomes.
  • Operational decisions where AI materially affects service levels or error rates.
  • Key safeguards: human-in-the-loop points, overrides, escalation paths, and model monitoring.
Form
How the Ledger was used
  • A short, referenceable record attached to Board Risk Committee papers.
  • A shared object between risk, technology, and compliance teams when planning new AI initiatives.
  • A concrete artefact that could be used in discussions with supervisors and funders.
  • The starting slice for an internal, living Trust Ledger that could be extended in later phases.
Key idea: the Trust Ledger slice is small on purpose – it gives the institution a concrete starting point that can be refreshed and extended, rather than a one-off report that ages on a shelf.
Signals

What changed as a result

In a live case, outcomes would be quantified. Here, the focus is on the kinds of signals partners typically look for.

Board & committees
Governance and oversight outcomes

• Board Risk Committee received a clear view of where AI was materially affecting key decisions.

• Committees agreed a small set of recurring AI questions anchored to the Trust Ledger.

• The Trust Brief became a reference point for forward-looking risk appetite discussions on AI.

Executives & supervisors
Executive and supervisory outcomes

• Executives gained a single artefact they could reuse in regulator or funder discussions.

• Internal teams could map new AI initiatives to the Ledger rather than starting from an empty page.

• Supervisory conversations shifted from “where are you using AI?” to “how are you governing these decisions?”

Strategic effect: AI governance became a tangible, recurring part of the board and supervisory conversation, not an occasional theme slide.
Fit

When this case pattern is relevant

The pattern behind Case Bank 1 is most relevant for institutions in a similar position.

  • You have multiple AI initiatives, but no single, board-ready view of where AI is genuinely material.
  • You expect more pointed questions from supervisors, investors, or funders within the next 12–18 months.
  • You prefer to build on what already exists – not to deploy a new platform just to describe AI governance.
  • You want a pilot that is concrete and time-bound, with clear options for what comes next.
