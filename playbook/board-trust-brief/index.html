Board Trust Brief

How boards use a 6-week AI Trust Brief.

This note outlines how a 6-week AI Trust Brief fits into the board and committee cycle – turning scattered AI artefacts into a short, reusable lens on where AI is material, which safeguards matter, and what needs to change next.

Audience

Who this playbook is for

Written for stewards who are responsible for AI oversight, but do not want to turn the board pack into a model catalogue.

  • Board chairs and committee chairs (risk, audit, technology, conduct).
  • Corporate secretaries responsible for board cycles and pack design.
  • Chief Risk Officers, Chief Operating Officers, and equivalent stewards.
  • Public or mission-driven entities facing funder or supervisory questions about AI.

The focus is narrow: where AI intersects with decisions that move money, change exposures, or materially affect customers and citizens.

In one line
Purpose of the Trust Brief

To give the board a clear, short view of: where AI matters, which safeguards exist, and what governance moves are needed in the next 12 months – without drowning anyone in technical detail.

From noise to signal

Before and after a Trust Brief

Before
Fragmented AI visibility

• AI appears in multiple slides, strategies, and project updates with different levels of detail.

• Logs, dashboards, and technical artefacts exist, but they are not curated for board-level use.

• Supervisors or funders ask for evidence of governance; responses are assembled ad-hoc each time.

After
A reusable board lens

• A short AI Trust Brief highlights 3–10 material AI decisions, their safeguards, and key gaps.

• A first Trust Ledger sample becomes an attachment to board packs and audit files.

• Future AI questions (from boards or supervisors) can be answered by updating the Ledger, not starting from scratch.

In simple terms: the Trust Brief distils the AI estate into a small number of decisions and safeguards that the board can actually act on, and the Trust Ledger keeps that view alive across cycles.
Board cycle

Using the Trust Brief in practice

A Trust Brief is most effective when it is explicitly anchored to the board and committee calendar.

1. Commission and scoping.

The chair, committee, or executive sponsor agrees the scope: which lines of business or AI-enabled processes are in focus, which regulatory or funder pressures matter, and what timeframe is relevant.

2. Six-week Trust Brief.

Over six weeks, Noetfield maps material AI use cases, reviews existing safeguards and artefacts, and drafts a short AI Trust Brief plus a first Trust Ledger entry or small set of entries.

3. Board / committee session.

The Trust Brief and Ledger sample are tabled in a scheduled session, typically alongside risk, conduct, or technology reports. The goal is clarity and prioritisation, not technical debate.

4. Actions and ownership.

The board agrees which items need executive follow-up (e.g., policy changes, testing, reporting, or investments) and where the Ledger should be extended over time.

5. Integration into pack design.

The corporate secretary and sponsors decide how often the Trust Ledger should appear in board packs or committee packs – for example, as a standing annex for material AI decisions.

6. Periodic refresh.

Ahead of major board cycles, regulatory reviews, or strategic decisions, the Trust Brief can be updated and the Ledger refreshed, rather than starting new AI slide-ware each time.

Signals, not theatre

Questions that make a Trust Brief useful

The value of a Trust Brief increases when boards ask a small set of repeatable questions, cycle after cycle.

  • Where, today, can AI move risk, money, or customers without clear board-level visibility?
  • Which AI-enabled decisions are already subject to explicit safeguards and human oversight – and which are not?
  • What evidence exists today that these safeguards are working as intended?
  • How does this view align with regulatory expectations or supervisory guidance in our sector?
  • What are the one to three governance moves we should expect management to deliver before the next major cycle?
Working principle: a small number of persistent questions, anchored to a Trust Ledger, are more effective than a new set of AI slides at every meeting.
