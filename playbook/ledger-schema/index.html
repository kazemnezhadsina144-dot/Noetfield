Trust Ledger Schema

Inside a Trust Ledger entry.

This note outlines a conceptual schema for Trust Ledger entries: the fields that matter, a small worked example, and how this view supports boards, executives, and supervisors when AI becomes material.

Concept

A Trust Ledger entry in outline

The unit of the Trust Ledger is not “a model” but a material decision path where AI plays a role and governance can be exercised.

A typical Trust Ledger entry describes one decision or decision-path where AI is present and where risk, money, or people can be moved. The exact implementation can vary, but the following field families recur:

  • Identity and scope. A stable ID, a short title, and a description of the decision or decision-path.
  • Context and systems. Business context, channels, AI systems and models in the path, and key data sources.
  • Risk and materiality. Why this decision matters (risk, conduct, prudential, customer, citizen, or mission impact).
  • Safeguards and controls. Technical, process, and human safeguards applied to this decision path.
  • Evidence and links. Where logs, prompts, test results, policies, and minutes can be found.
  • Ownership and visibility. Who is accountable, when it was last reviewed, and which forums (board, audit, supervisors) can see it.
Design principle
Minimal but reusable

The schema is deliberately compact: enough structure that stewards and supervisors can read, compare, and track decisions over time – without turning the Ledger into another complete data warehouse.

Fields

Core fields in a Trust Ledger entry

Below is a conceptual field list. In practice, organisations adapt naming and mappings to their own policies and systems.

  • Decision_ID – Stable identifier for the decision path (e.g., TL-CL-LIMIT-001).
  • Decision_title – Short label (e.g., “Retail credit limit adjustment for existing customers”).
  • Decision_context – Plain-language description of what the decision does and where it sits in the business.
  • Business_owner – Function or role accountable for the decision (not the model).
  • AI_role – How AI participates (e.g., “recommender”, “score input”, “automated decision with human override”).
  • Systems_and_models – Key systems, models, or vendors involved in this path.
  • Data_profile – High-level summary of key data categories (e.g., financial, behavioural, demographic, sensitive).
  • Risk_classification – Internal classification or external regime (e.g., high-risk AI under a given framework).
  • Safeguards_applied – Key controls and safeguards (testing, limits, overrides, escalation paths).
  • Human_oversight – Where and how humans can intervene, challenge, or override AI-influenced outcomes.
  • Evidence_links – Pointers to logs, test artefacts, policy documents, minutes, or tickets.
  • Board_and_supervisor_visibility – Which forums see this (board, committee, internal audit, regulator, funder).
  • Issues_and_actions – Known issues, observations, and agreed mitigation actions.
  • Last_reviewed_at – Date of last governance review or update.
  • Entry_owner – Role accountable for maintaining this Ledger entry.
Working view: if a field does not improve clarity or governance decisions, it can remain outside the Ledger and live in underlying systems instead.
Example

Illustrative Trust Ledger entry (simplified)

The example below is illustrative and uses generic language. Real entries would reference internal names, IDs, and policies. 

Decision_ID: TL-CL-LIMIT-001
Decision_title: Retail credit limit adjustment for existing customers

Decision_context:
  Automated and semi-automated adjustments of credit card limits for existing retail customers,
  triggered during periodic reviews and in response to specific customer requests.

Business_owner: Head of Retail Credit Risk
AI_role: Score input into limit recommendation engine (human-in-the-loop for high-impact cases)

Systems_and_models:
  - Internal credit risk model V3 (in-house)
  - Limit recommendation engine (core banking integration)
  - Customer servicing portal (branch and contact centre)

Data_profile:
  - Financial and transactional data
  - Behavioural repayment patterns
  - Limited demographic information (no sensitive health or biometric data)

Risk_classification:
  - Material prudential and conduct impact
  - Classified as "High materiality AI" under internal policy
  - In scope for upcoming supervisory review on AI-enabled credit decisions

Safeguards_applied:
  - Pre-deployment model validation and backtesting
  - Limit caps by risk band and product type
  - Automated flags for out-of-pattern recommendations
  - Mandatory human review above defined exposure thresholds

Human_oversight:
  - Credit officers must review and approve recommendations above certain limits
  - Manual override available in all channels with rationale logging
  - Quarterly portfolio review by Credit Risk Committee

Evidence_links:
  - Model validation report: GOV-DOC-2024-017
  - Policy: "AI-Enabled Credit Decisions v2.1"
  - Audit log location: [internal system reference]
  - Committee minutes: CRC-2024-Q2, CRC-2024-Q3

Board_and_supervisor_visibility:
  - Summarised in quarterly risk dashboard to Board Risk Committee
  - Included in AI Trust Brief for 2025 supervisory dialogue

Issues_and_actions:
  - Monitoring uplift in approvals for specific customer segments under review
  - Action: Enhance explainability tooling for borderline decisions (target: Q3 2025)

Last_reviewed_at: 2025-09-12
Entry_owner: Retail Credit Governance Lead
Key point: the Ledger does not replace technical documentation or policies; it points to them and gives stewards a stable, readable frame for oversight.
Alignment

How the schema supports regulatory and policy expectations

While regimes differ, many supervisory expectations converge on a few themes that the Trust Ledger is designed to support.

  • Clarity on use cases. Regulators increasingly expect a clear view of where AI is used in material decisions.
  • Documentation and traceability. Decision-relevant documentation, logs, and testing artefacts should be traceable.
  • Accountability and ownership. It should be clear who is responsible for governance of AI-enabled decisions.
  • Human oversight and safeguards. High-impact decisions should have defined oversight and escalation paths.
Governance lens
Not a legal opinion

The Ledger is a governance artefact, not a legal interpretation. It helps boards and executives see where regulatory themes land in practice – and offers supervisors a structured view of how those decisions are being managed.

Implementation

From schema to living Ledger

No single technical implementation is required. What matters is that the Ledger can be read, maintained, and reused across governance cycles.

  • Some organisations begin in a governed document or spreadsheet, then migrate to internal tooling.
  • Others embed the Ledger fields into existing risk or policy systems, surfacing a view for boards and committees.
  • In all cases, the Trust Brief is a practical starting point: it identifies the first 3–10 decisions that deserve entries.
Pragmatic path: start with a small, curated set of Ledger entries coming out of a Trust Brief, and extend only where governance and supervision clearly benefit.
