Board-level frame for AI trust
Mandate

A clear mandate for AI trust and governance.

This mandate describes how Noetfield shows up in AI governance work: where it operates, what it commits to, and what remains with boards, executives, and regulators. It is designed to be read alongside the Trust Brief, the Trust Ledger, and internal governance charters.

Why this exists

Why a mandate is needed

A concise mandate keeps AI governance work focused, non-theatrical, and aligned with real fiduciary duties.

AI governance conversations can drift into two unhelpful extremes: inflated promises that nobody can operationalise, or checklists that look impressive but do not change decisions. Both create risk for stewards and confusion for operators.

The Noetfield Mandate exists to provide a board-level frame that is:

  • Concrete enough to inform real decisions, audits, and supervision.
  • Narrow enough to avoid competing with internal teams, legal counsel, or vendors.
  • Reusable across different sectors and regulatory regimes.

It is not a legal charter and does not replace internal governance. It is a practical lens for how Noetfield contributes to AI trust.

In one view
The Mandate in three lines

• Focus on material AI decisions, not hype or experiments for their own sake.

• Turn existing artefacts into board- and supervisor-ready Trust Briefs and Ledgers.

• Clarify boundaries so responsibilities stay where they belong: with boards and executives.

In one sentence: the Noetfield Mandate is to make the governance of material AI decisions visible, traceable, and usable for real stewardship – without diluting board and executive accountability.
Mandate statement

What Noetfield is mandated to do

A focused mandate that can be quoted or embedded in internal governance documents.

Within engagements, Noetfield is mandated to:

  • Identify and map material AI decisions – where AI can move risk, money, customers, or regulatory exposure.
  • Surface and document the safeguards, controls, and accountabilities that already exist around those decisions.
  • Translate that reality into a 6-week AI Trust Brief and an evolving Trust Ledger that boards, executives, and regulators can reuse.
  • Align the Trust Ledger with relevant frameworks (sectoral guidance, regulation, internal policies) without claiming to replace them.
  • Flag gaps, tensions, and blind spots in a way that is practical and non-theatrical – focused on decisions and behaviour, not just documentation.
Use in practice
How boards can use this mandate

• As supporting language in AI governance charters or committee terms of reference.
• As context when commissioning a Trust Brief or Trust Ledger pilot.
• As a reference in discussions with auditors, supervisors, or funders about Noetfield’s role.

Boundaries

What Noetfield will not do

Clarity on boundaries is part of governance. Noetfield’s mandate is deliberately narrow.

To preserve clear lines of accountability, Noetfield’s mandate explicitly excludes:

  • Acting as a decision-maker for production AI systems, customer outcomes, or risk limits.
  • Replacing legal counsel, regulatory relations, or internal audit.
  • Serving as a trading venue, investment product, or fund manager.
  • Becoming the sole system of record for AI operations or technical telemetry.
  • Competing with internal technical teams or vendors as an AI development lab.

Noetfield sits beside these functions as a governance lens and a Trust Ledger that they can rely on – not above them.

Residual responsibility
What remains with boards and executives

• Setting and owning the organisation’s AI risk appetite and strategy.
• Approving and overseeing material AI deployments and their safeguards.
• Engaging with regulators, auditors, and funders on behalf of the institution.
• Ensuring resourcing, incentives, and culture support responsible AI behaviour.
