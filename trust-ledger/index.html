What the Trust Ledger is

The Trust Ledger is a disciplined, plain-language record of a small number of AI decisions that are truly material to your organisation: the systems that can move money, affect customers, shift prices, or trigger regulatory scrutiny.

Each entry captures the decision itself, the data and safeguards it relied on, and the people and committees who approved or challenged it. It does not replace technical documentation or monitoring. Instead, it compresses them into a narrative that board members, regulators, and supervisors can read in minutes – and use to ask better questions.

A single page of Trust Ledger entries can be attached to board packs, audit files, and supervisory engagements as evidence of how AI is actually governed, not just deployed.

Why boards and regulators need a Trust Ledger

The pressure is no longer just “Do you use AI?” – it is “How are AI decisions governed?”

Logs, dashboards, and model cards were designed for engineers. Board packs and policies were designed for committees. Incidents often live in yet another system. When something goes wrong – or when a regulator asks for evidence – organisations are forced to reconstruct a story from fragments.

The Trust Ledger gives you that story in advance. It connects the technical artefacts you already have to the fiduciary responsibilities you already hold. For each material AI decision, the ledger makes it clear:

  • What decision was taken – in language a non-technical director can understand.
  • Which data, models, and assumptions it relied on.
  • Which safeguards and limits were applied, and how they are monitored.
  • Who is accountable – and where the board or committees were actually in the loop.

How the Trust Ledger works alongside your existing controls

Noetfield does not replace your existing controls. The Trust Ledger sits as a thin governance layer above them, using what you already maintain:

  • Model documentation and validation reports.
  • Risk registers, control catalogues, and policies.
  • Monitoring dashboards and alerts.
  • Incident tickets and post-mortems.
  • Board and committee minutes.

For each material AI use case, we connect to these sources, extract what matters at the governance level, and compress it into one or two structured entries. The detailed artefacts remain where they belong; the Trust Ledger links back to them for audit and supervision.

Glossary – core Trust Ledger terms
Trust Ledger
A curated, plain-language record of critical AI decisions, safeguards, and accountability, designed for boards, regulators, and internal audit – built on top of existing technical and risk controls.
Critical AI decision
A decision influenced or executed by AI that is capable of moving money, affecting customers, impacting markets, or triggering meaningful regulatory or reputational risk.
Material AI use case
A cluster of AI-enabled processes that is significant enough, in scale or sensitivity, that it should be visible at board and committee level – not only in technical teams.
Safeguard
A constraint, limit, or monitoring practice applied to an AI system to prevent or reduce harm, including thresholds, overrides, human-in-the-loop steps, and automated rollbacks.
Governance layer
The structures, documents, and processes that connect technical systems to decision-makers with fiduciary duties – including boards, committees, and regulators.
Real-economy AI
AI systems deployed in financial services, infrastructure, utilities, healthcare, and other sectors where decisions materially affect customers, citizens, or markets.
Board-level AI incident
An event involving AI or automation that requires notification to the board or committees due to its regulatory, financial, operational, or reputational impact.

FAQ – the Trust Ledger in practice

How is a Trust Ledger different from model documentation?
Model documentation is written by and for technical teams. It describes architectures, datasets, training runs, and validation details. The Trust Ledger is written for stewards – boards, executives, regulators, and internal audit. It references the technical artefacts, but focuses on decisions, safeguards, accountability, and how those decisions were surfaced to governance bodies.
Is the Trust Ledger a piece of software or a document?
In the current phase, the Trust Ledger is delivered as a structured, board-ready report with clearly defined entries and links back to your systems. Over time, Noetfield is building infrastructure so that ledger entries can be maintained and queried as a live governance layer above your existing controls.
How many entries should a Trust Ledger contain?
The Ledger is intentionally sparse. Most organisations start with 3–10 entries that correspond to their most material AI decisions. As the organisation matures, additional entries can be added, but the goal is always clarity, not exhaustiveness.
Who maintains the Trust Ledger inside the organisation?
Ownership usually sits where risk and technology meet – for example, with a Chief Risk Officer, Chief Data or AI Officer, or a central governance function. Noetfield helps define ownership explicitly so that updates are tied to real roles, not anonymous inboxes.
Can the Trust Ledger support regulatory and supervisory engagements?
Yes. A concise Ledger page attached to supervisory communications, thematic reviews, or on-site visits gives regulators a clear view of your most critical AI decisions, how they were governed, and where board oversight appears in the chain. It reduces the need to reconstruct that story under time pressure.
